A History of Modern Cryptography Law in the United States

World War II: The Beginning of a New Cryptographic Era

During World War II, Nazi Germany desigend and extensively used Enigma machines and Lorenz ciphers to conceal military communications. Polish mathmeticians Marian Rejewski, Jerzy Różycki, and Henryk Zygalski and British mathmeticians Alan Turing and Gordon Welchman used advanced techniques to crack Enigma's encryption and decrypt German military communications. They devised a mechanical machine known as a bombe to greatly accelerate the process of decrypting German messages. Ever since, machines and algorithms that protect and deprotect messages have exploded in capabilities and power.

Regulations in the 20th Century

The U.S. has never restricted cryptographic use within the States. However, beginning in the early 1900s, the U.S. has been strict about export of cryptographic technology. From 1954 until 1996, encryption technology was included in the U.S. Munitions List. During the Cold War, The U.S. was careful that the Eastern bloc did not gain access to such technology. The Trading with the Enemy Act of 1917, the Export Control Act of 1949 and others allowed the President to regulate nearly all exports. The International Emergency Economic Powers Act of 1977 succeeded these, requiring the President to declare an emergency. However, since there are few limits to the President's ability to declare an emergency, this is a weak limitation and has often been abused.

In the 1990s, with the advent of PCs, cryptographic regulations became challenged at the indivdual level. Technologies such as PGP and SSL (predecessor of TLS) were firmly regulated.

PGP's original author, Phil Zimmermann was placed under criminal investigation when PGP made its way outside of the U.S.. Zimmermann fought back by publishing PGP's entire source code in a book.

SSL was divided into a U.S. edition and and a much weaker international edition.

The Export Control Reform Act of 2018 and Export Administration Regulations

The Export Control Reform Act of 2018 authorizes the President to control the export, reexport, and transfer of commodities, software, and technology to protect the national security, and to promote the foreign policy, of the United States, and for other purposes. This is the legal basis for the BIS's Export Administration Regulations (EAR).

The EAR requires a license for the export of some cryptographic technology. The power to grant and deny these licenses lies in the BIS. However, according to the BIS, publicly available encryption source code is not subject to the EAR once a notification email is sent to the BIS and NSA. In order to publish TurTLS on the Internet, I was required to send a notification email.
